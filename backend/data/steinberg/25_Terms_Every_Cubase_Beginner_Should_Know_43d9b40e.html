25 Terms Every Cubase Beginner Should Know

Your first few weeks in music production — made simpler, one word at a time.

So, you’ve installed Cubase, opened your first project, and… there it is: a whole new world of buttons, menus, and mysterious terms.

Don’t worry — you’re not alone. Every music producer started somewhere. This glossary is here to help you learn the language of Cubase, so you can focus on what matters most: making music.

Whether you’re dragging in loops, recording your first melody, or trying to figure out what a “send” is, this guide will explain the most important words you’ll hear in your first weeks with Cubase.

Let’s get started.

Project & Track Basics

Project

Your full song file — including all tracks, audio, MIDI, instruments, effects, and more. Every session you work on is a project.

Track

A lane in your project that holds either audio or MIDI data. For example, one track might hold a vocal, another a drum loop, another a synth.

Timeline

The horizontal ruler at the top of the screen that shows time in beats, bars, or seconds. This is where you build and arrange your song.

Event

A block of content on a track — could be a recording, a loop, or a MIDI part. You can move, copy, trim, and edit it.

Loop

A repeating piece of music — often a short beat, chord progression, or melody. Cubase makes looping super easy with drag-and-drop sample support.

Sample

A short audio file, like a snare hit or a vocal phrase. You can bring in samples from your own collection or use libraries and packs.

Recording & Playback

Record Arm

Before recording on a track, you need to “arm” it — so Cubase knows where to place the recording.

Monitoring

Want to hear your voice or guitar while recording? Turn on monitoring to listen to your input live.

Metronome (Click)

The steady beat that keeps you in time while recording. You can turn it on/off in the Transport Bar.

Latency

A small delay between playing something and hearing it back. Using an audio interface and adjusting your buffer size helps reduce latency.

Transport Controls

Play, stop, record, rewind, loop — these are your main playback buttons, located at the bottom of the screen.

MIDI & Virtual Instruments

MIDI

Musical data that tells instruments what notes to play, for how long, how loud, and more. MIDI doesn’t make sound on its own — it controls virtual instruments.

Instrument Track

A track that combines MIDI input and a virtual instrument (like a synth or piano). It’s your go-to for creating melodies and beats.

VST Instrument (VSTi)

A software instrument that lives inside Cubase. Examples include Retrologue, Groove Agent, or third-party synths like Serum.

Piano Roll / Key Editor

A grid where you can draw, move, and edit MIDI notes. You’ll probably spend a lot of time here!

Chord Pads

Create rich chords with a single click — even if you don’t know music theory. A great feature for building progressions fast.

Scale Assistant

Keeps your MIDI notes in the right key, so you always sound in tune — perfect if you’re just starting out.

Mixing & Effects

MixConsole

The part of Cubase where you adjust volume, panning, and effects for each track — just like a physical mixing board.

Volume Fader

Controls how loud a track is. You’ll find this in both the Track Inspector and the MixConsole.

Pan

Moves the sound left or right in the stereo field — for example, guitar slightly left, piano slightly right.

Insert Effects

Effects (like reverb, delay, or EQ) added directly to one track.

Send Effects

Effects shared across multiple tracks using an FX Channel. Saves CPU and makes your mix more consistent.

Automation

Automatically change things like volume or effects over time — think fade-ins, filter sweeps, or muting a track at just the right moment.

Bonus: Handy Extras

Render / Export

Once your song is done, export it to create an audio file (like MP3 or WAV) you can share.

Latency Compensation

Cubase keeps everything in sync, even if a plugin adds a slight delay. Magic, right?

Quantize

Snaps your MIDI or audio to the grid so everything stays in time.

Tip for Beginners

Don’t feel pressured to learn everything at once.

Start small. Loop a beat. Add a chord. Record a vocal idea.

This glossary will be here when you need it — one step at a time.

Everyone starts somewhere. And your first track is just a few clicks away.
