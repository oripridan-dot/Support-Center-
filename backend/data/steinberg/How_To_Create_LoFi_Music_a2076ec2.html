What Is LoFi And Why Is It So Popular?

LoFi was originally the technical opposite to HiFi, but these days also a musical genre. This hip hop-based sound fully embraces artifacts in music production that vanished years ago, when computers became the main tool for most beat producers.

DAW solutions, and audio interfaces, in many ways democratized music making. Now, even home producers could produce tracks in true HiFi glory. Back in the late nineties who could have imagined that we would go totally nostalgic two decades later.

The grit and noisiness create an intimate and honest, but also often rough, experience that many of us love. After years of (too) pure sounding beats we turn back to the wonderful era of noise, hiss, distortion, and vinyl crackles – only this time we achieve this in a fully digital environment. Let’s go!

LoFi Production Techniques

Back in the days, the reason behind the sound was nothing less than limitations of the equipment. Samplers used lower bit depth and sample rates (e.g. the E-mu SP-1200 used 12 bit, 26.040 kHz with 256 KB memory – allowing for a total of 10 seconds of sampling!).

On top of the actual instruments, we also need to remember that the music was recorded onto tape, giving a saturation that certainly contributes to the vibe of LoFi.

So, to recreate all these pleasant “drawbacks” of analog gear, we need to bring back elements of dirt into our digital domains. We’ve already mentioned tape saturation and the HALion based instruments sure deliver this but also other kinds of distortion, like bit reduction, tube drive and clipping. Be reducing the sample rate you will immediately get a rougher sound.

Melancholy is the key

While beat sure is important, a lounge nostalgia vibe is crucial in LoFi hip hop. Of course, you may use sample packs with jazzy electric piano chord progressions – but playing around with the free Steinberg LoFi Piano gives you more options and more direct control of your harmonies. (Plus: there are plenty of jazz chord tutorials on the web.)

By using the 6 tonal controls (Flutter, Compress, Saturate, Reduce, Filter and reverb), dialing in the dusty aura is easy as that!

Beat it!

Forget about those multi-sampled arena rock kits; this time sample and bit reduction, unorthodox use of filters and overall grittiness is our guide.

Programming wise, we will aim for a relaxed feel so starting with a standard backbeat snare (i.e. on 2 and 4) is a good start and then starting with a straight kick on 1. After his opening procedure, feel free to try out different patterns to get a basic beat going.

Now, since we aim for something as far away from electro-stiffness as possible, we need some elements that are allowed to be pretty off-beat: Enter the hihats!

Start off by entering an 8-note pattern (ghost-notes are allowed). Then, either start using different quantize settings in order to get the hits a wee bit late or by simply dragging them manually.

The groove should be pretty dusty and heavy and a good way to achieve this is by treating the whole drum bus with tape and vinyl like dynamics. The goal is to get that “way too heavy needle pressure” thing going.

Touch on bass

The bass part needs to be a bass, so go a bit easier on the aggressive types of effects here. Some overdrive and dirt is fine but since it should be glued together with the beat you may go more all-in with messy effects on the drum parts.

The LoFi genre mostly connects to the classical ways of approaching bass pars. This means that an electric bass sound may be a great way to start. The Steinberg Electric Bass comes with lots of realistic presets that will get you into the right mood.

A piece of advice is to make sure that the groove of the bass part is coherent with the drum pattern – but not too much. A certain loose drift in-between these two elements will breathe more life into the track.

Next level: MIDI to audio

After completing a bunch of instrumental clips, we may still feel that the arrangement feels somewhat MIDI-esque with instruments ringing out in a (too) natural way. To get a more sample loop style we could try fiddling with gates and automation but a more efficient way – and much more fun – is to simply turn your MIDI-parts into samples. After this you may slice them up for another creative round in the Goove Agent environment.

By cutting the chord parts, bass lines and the complete drum loops, into pieces and placing them on different drum cells we are ready for a classical MPC bonanza. And don’t forget to save the loops in your own libraries for future use. “Stealing” samples from yourself in future projects can be a real boost for the inspiration!

LoFi In Cubase

Cubase offers a perfect palette to get the right Lo Fi grit in your tracks. Effect plugins like e.g. AmpSimulator, Bitcrusher, DaTube, Grungelizer, Amp Racks, Quadrafuzz v2 and Distroyer get you covered – from gentle colouring to complete sonic destruction.
